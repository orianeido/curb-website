Parking in Israel: The Complete Guide for Drivers and Visitors

June 1, 2026 · 9 min read · Updated for 2026
images/blog/parking-guide-israel-hero.webp

Parking in Israel can feel confusing the first time you see a curb painted in blue and white, red and white, or no color at all. Each combination means something different, and the rules can change depending on the city, the time of day, and whether it's Shabbat or a holiday. This guide breaks down everything you need to know before you park — whether you're a local driver or visiting with a rental car.

Quick answer

Understanding curb colors and parking signs

Israeli curbs are color-coded, and learning the basic combinations will get you most of the way to a confident parking decision:

  • Blue and white: Paid parking during the hours shown on the nearby sign — usually controlled by Pango, Cellopark, or a meter.
  • Red and white: Stopping and parking are prohibited at all times, even briefly.
  • Gray or unpainted curb: Generally free parking, but always check for a posted sign — some areas restrict parking to residents with permits.
  • Yellow markings: Reserved for buses, taxis, or loading zones depending on the accompanying sign.

Signs often include small print specifying days and hours — for example, paid parking might apply only Sunday–Thursday, 8:00–19:00, and be free at all other times.

Paid parking zones and how to pay

Most Israeli cities use one or more mobile payment apps instead of traditional meters. The two most common are:

  • Pango: Widely used across Tel Aviv, Jerusalem, Haifa, and most major cities.
  • Cellopark: Common alternative, sometimes the only option on certain streets.

Both apps let you start and stop a parking session remotely, so you only pay for the time you actually use. Look for a small sign or sticker on the pole indicating which provider(s) are accepted on that street, and double-check the zone code before starting your session.

Free parking on Shabbat and holidays

In most Israeli cities, paid parking on blue-and-white curbs is free from Friday afternoon through the end of Shabbat on Saturday night, and on Jewish holidays. The exact start and end times can vary by municipality, so it's worth checking the sign rather than assuming. Red-and-white "no parking" curbs remain in effect regardless of the day.

Rule of thumb: color tells you the type of zone, the small print tells you when it applies. When in doubt, scan the sign with Curb for an instant answer.

Restricted, loading, and disabled parking zones

Beyond the basic color system, watch for these common zone types:

  • Resident-only zones: Marked with a sign showing a permit requirement, often in dense residential neighborhoods.
  • Loading zones: Time-limited, typically restricted to commercial vehicles during business hours.
  • Disabled parking: Marked with the wheelchair symbol and require a valid Israeli or recognized international disabled parking permit displayed on the dashboard.

Tips for tourists and rental car drivers

  • Download Pango or Cellopark before you start driving — both support international phone numbers and credit cards.
  • Keep small change on hand for areas with older meters, especially in smaller towns.
  • Avoid red-and-white curbs entirely — fines are common and towing happens quickly in busy areas.
  • If a sign is only in Hebrew, look for the universal color cues (blue/white = pay, red/white = no parking) as a starting point, then confirm with a translation app or Curb.

FAQ

Common questions about parking in Israel

What do the curb colors mean in Israel?

Blue-and-white curbs mean paid parking during posted hours. Red-and-white curbs mean stopping and parking are forbidden. Gray or unmarked curbs are generally free, subject to posted signs.

Is parking free on Shabbat?

In most cities, paid parking is free from Friday afternoon through Saturday night and on Jewish holidays — but always check the posted hours, since they vary by city.

Which app do I need to pay for parking?

Pango and Cellopark are the most common. Many streets accept both, but some are limited to one provider — check the sign or nearby signage for the accepted apps.

Can I use these apps with a rental car and foreign number?

Yes. Both Pango and Cellopark support sign-up with international phone numbers and credit cards, so visitors can use them just like local drivers.
